Background

I started doing web development for other people in 2006. I was in high school, and jQuery wasn't a thing yet.

After graduating from Carnegie Mellon in 2011, I started a software company that used NLP to make public information more accessible online. The startup was unable to find product-market fit, but it left me with a strong sense that building software was something I was good at and something I really enjoyed.

To really learn the craft, I spent time working in digital agencies like R/GA and Smashing Boxes. There I gained experience as both a project manager and as an engineer while working for big clients like Quintiles, Walmart, and Nike.

After improving my skills, I was ready for another challenge. I moved to California to work on a few startups, as both a co-founder and an early employee.

In 2015 I decided to move back to Pennsylvania to be closer to family. Since there were no software companies in my town, I joined a manufacturing company. I was delighted to find many complex problems that could be solved with software. I was also humbled by the domain knowledge of the people I worked with.

At GapVax, I began to practice a bottom-up approach for collaborating across departments to build systems that benefit the entire company. This is a skill that I have continued to improve throughout my career, and is something that I view as a key part of my role as a software engineering leader.

Over the last decade, I have gained an incredible breadth of experience in both software and business. I excel at rapid prototyping, listening to people, and designing complex systems that meet the needs of everyone involved. I feel that my passion for the work, my willingness to go the extra mile, and my faith make me a valuable addition to any team.

Backend

I've got production experience with a handful of backend frameworks, like .NET, Phoenix, Flask, and Rails. If I had to pick one, I'd choose Phoenix because of its processes and built-in distributed pubsub.

I don't have strong opinions on REST vs. GraphQL vs RPC, but I do prefer SQL over NoSQL for persistence in most cases.

I've got a ton of experience writing SQL, and I prefer Postgres. When possible, I like to move business logic into the database through triggers, views, and functions. I'm also a big advocate for row-level-security.

In terms of improving performance, I try to make the solution match the problem. Lately I've been using Redis and BullMQ for caching and message queues.
